Workout: Finite vs Infinite Geometric Sums

Student: Justin Koo Date: 2026-05-03
Standard: CCSS.MATH.CONTENT.HSA-SSE.B.4 — Derive and use formulas for finite/infinite geometric series; identify when an infinite series converges.
Two formulas — DON'T mix them up

Finite ($k=0$ to $n$):   $\displaystyle\sum_{k=0}^{n} r^k = \frac{1 - r^{\,n+1}}{1 - r}$   — works for any $r \ne 1$.

Infinite ($k=0$ to $\infty$):   $\displaystyle\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} r^k = \frac{1}{1 - r}$   — exists only when $|r| < 1$. If $|r| \ge 1$, the series diverges.

Problem i — Finite, $|r|<1$
$\displaystyle \sum_{k=0}^{10} \left(\tfrac{2}{3}\right)^k$
finite formula 11 terms (k=0..10)

Apply finite formula with $r = \tfrac{2}{3}$, $n = 10$:

$\displaystyle S = \frac{1 - (2/3)^{11}}{1 - 2/3} = \frac{1 - (2/3)^{11}}{1/3} = 3\!\left(1 - \tfrac{2^{11}}{3^{11}}\right) = 3\!\left(1 - \tfrac{2048}{177147}\right) = 3 \cdot \tfrac{175099}{177147} = \tfrac{175099}{59049}$

Decimal: $\;(2/3)^{11} \approx 0.01156$,   $S \approx 3(1 - 0.01156) \approx 2.9653$.

$S = 3\!\left(1 - \left(\tfrac{2}{3}\right)^{11}\right) = \dfrac{175099}{59049} \approx 2.9653$
Problem ii — Infinite, $|r|<1$ (CONVERGES)
$\displaystyle \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \left(\tfrac{2}{3}\right)^k$
infinite formula |r|<1 ✓

$|r| = 2/3 < 1$ ✓  ⇒  series converges. Use $\displaystyle S_\infty = \tfrac{1}{1-r}$:

$\displaystyle S_\infty = \frac{1}{1 - 2/3} = \frac{1}{1/3} = 3$

Sanity check vs (i): $S_{10} \approx 2.9653$ is just shy of $3$. The "missing" $\approx 0.0347$ is the tail $\sum_{k=11}^{\infty}(2/3)^k$. The partial sum is climbing toward $3$. ✓

$S_\infty = 3$
Problem iii — Finite, $|r|>1$ (still works!)
$\displaystyle \sum_{k=0}^{10} \left(\tfrac{4}{3}\right)^k$
finite formula |r|>1 OK for finite

$|r| = 4/3 > 1$ — but for a finite sum that doesn't matter. Apply the formula:

$\displaystyle S = \frac{1 - (4/3)^{11}}{1 - 4/3} = \frac{1 - (4/3)^{11}}{-1/3} = 3\!\left((4/3)^{11} - 1\right) = 3\!\left(\tfrac{4^{11}}{3^{11}} - 1\right)$

$\;= 3 \cdot \dfrac{4^{11} - 3^{11}}{3^{11}} = \dfrac{4^{11} - 3^{11}}{3^{10}} = \dfrac{4194304 - 177147}{59049} = \dfrac{4017157}{59049}$

Decimal: $\;(4/3)^{11} \approx 23.679$,   $S \approx 3(23.679 - 1) \approx 68.04$.

$S = 3\!\left(\!\left(\tfrac{4}{3}\right)^{11} - 1\right) = \dfrac{4017157}{59049} \approx 68.04$
Problem iv — Infinite, $|r|>1$ (DIVERGES)
$\displaystyle \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \left(\tfrac{4}{3}\right)^k$
infinite formula |r|>1 ✗

$|r| = 4/3 > 1$ ⇒   the terms $(4/3)^k$ grow without bound as $k \to \infty$ — the partial sums explode.

The formula $\tfrac{1}{1-r} = \tfrac{1}{1-4/3} = -3$ is nonsense here — it would say a sum of positive terms equals a negative number. The formula is only valid when $|r|<1$.

DIVERGES
Problem v — Finite, $r = -\tfrac{4}{3}$ (signs alternate, still computable)
$\displaystyle \sum_{k=0}^{10} \left(-\tfrac{4}{3}\right)^k$
finite formula alternating signs

Apply the finite formula with $r = -\tfrac{4}{3}$, $n = 10$. Note $(-4/3)^{11}$ has odd power, so the sign is negative: $\;(-4/3)^{11} = -(4/3)^{11}$.

$\displaystyle S = \frac{1 - (-4/3)^{11}}{1 - (-4/3)} = \frac{1 + (4/3)^{11}}{1 + 4/3} = \frac{1 + (4/3)^{11}}{7/3} = \frac{3}{7}\left(1 + (4/3)^{11}\right)$

$\;= \dfrac{3}{7} \cdot \dfrac{3^{11} + 4^{11}}{3^{11}} = \dfrac{3^{11} + 4^{11}}{7 \cdot 3^{10}} = \dfrac{177147 + 4194304}{7 \cdot 59049} = \dfrac{4371451}{413343}$

Decimal: $\;S \approx \tfrac{3}{7}(1 + 23.679) \approx \tfrac{3}{7} \cdot 24.679 \approx 10.577$.

$S = \dfrac{3}{7}\!\left(1 + \left(\tfrac{4}{3}\right)^{11}\right) = \dfrac{4371451}{413343} \approx 10.58$
Problem vi — Infinite, $|r| = \tfrac{4}{3} > 1$ (DIVERGES)
$\displaystyle \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \left(-\tfrac{4}{3}\right)^k$
infinite formula |r|>1 ✗

Even though $r$ is negative, what matters is $|r|$. Here $|r| = 4/3 > 1$, so the magnitudes $|(-4/3)^k| = (4/3)^k$ grow without bound. The partial sums oscillate between huge positive and huge negative numbers — no limit exists.

The formula $\tfrac{1}{1-(-4/3)} = \tfrac{1}{7/3} = \tfrac{3}{7}$ is not the sum — it's the value the formula spits out, but the formula doesn't apply.

DIVERGES
Pattern to remember
RatioFinite (k=0..10)Infinite (k=0..∞)
$r = 2/3$  ($|r|<1$)≈ 2.973 (converges)
$r = 4/3$  ($|r|>1$)≈ 68.0DIVERGES
$r = -4/3$  ($|r|>1$)≈ 10.6DIVERGES

Take-away: the finite formula $\tfrac{1-r^{n+1}}{1-r}$ never lies. The infinite formula $\tfrac{1}{1-r}$ is a limit shortcut that only exists when $r^n \to 0$, i.e. when $|r|<1$. Plugging $|r|\ge 1$ into it returns numbers, but those numbers don't represent any actual sum.
