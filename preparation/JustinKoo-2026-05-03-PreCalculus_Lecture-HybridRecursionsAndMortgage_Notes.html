Hybrid Recursions, Fixed Points & Mortgage Application

Student: Justin Koo Date: 2026-05-03
Standard: CCSS.MATH.CONTENT.HSF-BF.A.1 / HSF-LE.A.2 — Build recursive and explicit formulas for sequences modeling real-world phenomena (finance, biology, decay).
1. Concept Insights — Why "Hybrid"?
The recursion zoo

Most recursions you've seen come in two flavors:

  • Geometric ($b = 0$): $\;T_n = a \cdot T_{n-1}$.   Each term is a multiple of the last. (Compound interest with no payments.)
  • Arithmetic ($a = 1$): $\;T_n = T_{n-1} + b$.   Add a constant each step.

But the real world is messier. What if both happen — multiply by $a$ and add $b$? That's a hybrid recursion:

$\displaystyle T_n = a\,T_{n-1} + b, \quad T_0 \text{ given}, \quad a \ne 0,\; b \ne 0$

Real models that need this:

  • Biology: population grows by factor $a$ per year, then $b$ animals migrate in (or out).
  • Pharmacology: body retains fraction $a$ of drug each day, then patient takes a dose $b$.
  • Finance: loan balance grows by factor $a = 1+r$, then monthly payment $-p$ knocks it down.
The Explicit Formula — derived by unrolling

Iterate the recursion starting from $T_0$:

$T_1 = aT_0 + b$
$T_2 = a(aT_0+b)+b = a^2 T_0 + ab + b$
$T_3 = a^3 T_0 + a^2 b + ab + b$
$\;\vdots$
$T_n = a^n T_0 + b(1 + a + a^2 + \cdots + a^{n-1})$

The bracketed part is a finite geometric sum $= \dfrac{1-a^n}{1-a}$. So:

$\displaystyle T_n = a^n\,T_0 + b\cdot\frac{1-a^n}{1-a}$

First piece = "geometric growth of the seed." Second piece = "accumulated effect of all the $b$'s, geometrically discounted."

The Fixed Point (Equilibrium) — when nothing changes

Suppose the sequence ever reaches a value $T^*$ where the next term equals the previous: $T_n = T_{n-1} = T^*$. Plug in:

$T^* = a T^* + b \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; T^*(1 - a) = b \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; \boxed{\,T^* = \dfrac{b}{1-a}\,}$

Long-run behavior:

  • $|a|<1$: every starting $T_0$ funnels toward $T^*$ (stable equilibrium).
  • $|a|>1$: any deviation from $T^*$ blows up (unstable).
2. Worked Example — Simple
Example 1 — Find the fixed point and the closed-form formula
Let $T_0 = 100$ and $T_n = 0.5\,T_{n-1} + 30$. Find $T_5$ exactly, the explicit formula, and the long-run equilibrium.

Step 1 — Identify $a$ and $b$. $a = 0.5$, $b = 30$, $T_0 = 100$.

Step 2 — Equilibrium. $T^* = \dfrac{b}{1-a} = \dfrac{30}{1 - 0.5} = \dfrac{30}{0.5} = 60$.

Step 3 — Explicit formula. $$T_n = (0.5)^n(100) + 30 \cdot \frac{1 - (0.5)^n}{1 - 0.5} = (0.5)^n(100) + 60\big(1 - (0.5)^n\big)$$ Simplify: $T_n = 60 + (0.5)^n(100 - 60) = 60 + 40\,(0.5)^n$.

Step 4 — Plug $n=5$. $T_5 = 60 + 40\,(0.5)^5 = 60 + 40 \cdot \tfrac{1}{32} = 60 + 1.25 = 61.25$.

$T_n = 60 + 40\,(0.5)^n$,   $T_5 = 61.25$,   $T^* = 60$ (stable since $|0.5|<1$).

Notice the elegant form $T_n = T^* + (T_0 - T^*)a^n$ — every hybrid solution is "equilibrium + decaying offset." This is the most insightful way to remember it.

3. Worked Example — Harder (Mortgage Payment)
Example 2 — Paying off a 30-year, $500,000 mortgage at 6% APR
A $\$500{,}000$ home loan is to be paid off in $30$ years ($= 360$ monthly payments) at an annual interest rate of $r = 6\%$. The bank applies $\tfrac{r}{12} = 0.005$ monthly interest to the unpaid balance, then deducts the monthly payment $p$. Find $p$ so that the balance hits $0$ after 360 months.

Step 1 — Set up the recursion. Let $B_n$ be the balance after $n$ months. Each month: balance grows by $(1 + 0.005)$, then $p$ is paid: $$B_n = (1.005)\,B_{n-1} - p, \qquad B_0 = 500{,}000$$ Here $a = 1.005$, $b = -p$.

Step 2 — Apply the explicit formula. $$B_n = (1.005)^n (500{,}000) + (-p)\cdot \frac{1 - (1.005)^n}{1 - 1.005} = (1.005)^n (500{,}000) - p \cdot \frac{(1.005)^n - 1}{0.005}$$

Step 3 — Set $B_{360} = 0$ and solve for $p$. $$0 = (1.005)^{360}(500{,}000) - p \cdot \frac{(1.005)^{360} - 1}{0.005}$$ $$p = \frac{(1.005)^{360}(500{,}000) \cdot 0.005}{(1.005)^{360} - 1} = (500{,}000) \cdot \frac{0.005 \cdot (1.005)^{360}}{(1.005)^{360} - 1}$$

Step 4 — Numerics. $(1.005)^{360} \approx 6.0226$. So: $$p \approx 500{,}000 \cdot \frac{0.005 \cdot 6.0226}{6.0226 - 1} = 500{,}000 \cdot \frac{0.030113}{5.0226} \approx 500{,}000 \cdot 0.0059955 \approx 2997.75$$

Monthly payment $\boxed{p \approx \$2{,}997.75}$ for 360 months.

General mortgage formula (worth memorizing): $\;\; p = L \cdot \dfrac{(r/12)(1 + r/12)^{12y}}{(1 + r/12)^{12y} - 1}\;$ where $L$ = loan, $r$ = annual rate, $y$ = years.

2. For $T_0 = 10$ and $T_n = 0.8\,T_{n-1} + 6$, find the equilibrium $T^*$ and describe the long-run behavior of $T_n$.
ANSWER

$T^* = \dfrac{b}{1-a} = \dfrac{6}{1 - 0.8} = \dfrac{6}{0.2} = 30$.

Since $|a| = 0.8 < 1$, the equilibrium is stable. Starting from $T_0 = 10 < 30$, the sequence rises and approaches $30$ from below.

Closed form: $T_n = 30 + (10 - 30)(0.8)^n = 30 - 20(0.8)^n$. As $n \to \infty$, $\;T_n \to 30$.

$T^* = 30$,   $T_n \to 30$ (rises from 10, never exceeds 30)

3. Drug dosing model. Each day, the body retains $80\%$ of the drug already in the bloodstream, and the patient takes a fresh $50$ mg dose. Find the long-run steady-state concentration $T^*$ in mg.
ANSWER

Recursion: $T_n = 0.8\,T_{n-1} + 50$. So $a = 0.8$, $b = 50$.

$T^* = \dfrac{50}{1 - 0.8} = \dfrac{50}{0.2} = 250$ mg.

Stable since $|a|<1$ — regardless of the starting concentration, the level approaches $250$ mg.

Steady-state $= 250$ mg

Insight: the steady state is much higher than a single dose ($50$ mg) because residue accumulates. This is why doctors care about half-lives.

4. Car loan. $\$30{,}000$ borrowed for $5$ years ($= 60$ months) at annual rate $4.8\%$. Find the monthly payment $p$.
ANSWER

Monthly rate: $\tfrac{0.048}{12} = 0.004$. So $a = 1.004$, recursion $B_n = 1.004\,B_{n-1} - p$.

Set $B_{60} = 0$: $$p = 30{,}000 \cdot \frac{0.004 \cdot (1.004)^{60}}{(1.004)^{60} - 1}$$

$(1.004)^{60} \approx 1.27049$.   Numerator: $30{,}000 \cdot 0.004 \cdot 1.27049 \approx 152.46$.   Denom: $0.27049$.

$p \approx \dfrac{152.46}{0.27049} \approx 563.61$.

$p \approx \$563.61$ per month

5. Population with harvesting. A fish population grows by $5\%$ per year ($a = 1.05$), but $10$ fish are caught and removed at year-end ($b = -10$). Start with $T_0 = 200$ fish. Find $T^*$ and explain whether the population stabilizes, grows, or crashes — then compute $T_{10}$.
ANSWER

Equilibrium: $T^* = \dfrac{-10}{1 - 1.05} = \dfrac{-10}{-0.05} = 200$.

$T_0$ already equals $T^*$! So the population stays at exactly $200$ forever. Let's verify with the formula.

$T_n = T^* + (T_0 - T^*)a^n = 200 + (200 - 200)(1.05)^n = 200 + 0 = 200$ for all $n$.

$T_{10} = 200$.

$T^* = 200$,   $T_n = 200$ for all $n$ (perfectly balanced)

Stability warning: $|a| = 1.05 > 1$, so this equilibrium is unstable. If $T_0 = 199$ (just below), the population would slowly crash toward 0; if $T_0 = 201$, it would slowly grow without bound. Sustainable harvest is razor-thin.
