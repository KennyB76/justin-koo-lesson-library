Power Series, IoC, Σ Rules & Bernoulli Telescoping
Practice Set — 4 Items × 4 Questions

Student: Justin Koo Date: 2026-05-03
Standard: CCSS.MATH.CONTENT.HSF-BF.A.2 / HSA-SSE.B.4 — Recognize and rewrite series; identify intervals of convergence for parametric (power) series; manipulate finite sums.
★ Quick Reference (from lecture)
Geometric sum: $\displaystyle \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} g_0 \cdot r^k = \frac{g_0}{1-r}$ when $|r| < 1$. For a power series $\sum (r(x))^k$, the IoC is the set of $x$ where $|r(x)| < 1$. Bernoulli telescoping: split $\frac{1}{(\,\cdot\,)(\,\cdot\,)}$ via partial fractions, then cancel.
Item 1 · Power Series as a Family of Geometric Series
Directions: For each problem, identify $g_0$ and $r$ first, then answer the question. Plugging in a specific $x$ turns a power series into a number-only geometric series.
4 Questions — "Plug-in awareness"
Test: Can you spot $g_0$, $r$ in different forms (k starts at 0 vs 1, shifted center, fraction)?
1.1 For $\displaystyle \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \left(\tfrac{x}{2}\right)^k$, plug in $x = 1$. Does the resulting numerical series converge? If yes, what is its sum?
1.2 For $\displaystyle \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} (3x)^k$, give one value of $x$ that makes the series converge and one value that makes it diverge. Justify both.
1.3 Identify $r$ and $g_0$ for $\displaystyle \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \left(\frac{x-2}{4}\right)^k$.
1.4 For $\displaystyle \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \left(\frac{x+1}{3}\right)^k$, identify $g_0$ (be careful — the index starts at $k=1$, not $0$). What is $r$?
Item 2 · Find IoC and the Sum Function $P(x)$
Directions: Apply $|r(x)| < 1$ to find the IoC, then write the closed-form sum $\;P(x) = \dfrac{g_0}{1-r}$. Simplify so $P(x)$ has no fractions stacked inside fractions.
4 Questions — "IoC mechanics"
Includes: standard, shifted center, variable in denominator, and starting at $k=1$.
2.1 $\displaystyle \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \left(\frac{x}{5}\right)^k$. Find IoC and $P(x)$.
2.2 $\displaystyle \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \left(\frac{x-1}{3}\right)^k$. Find IoC and $P(x)$. (Center is shifted — solve $|x-1| < 3$.)
2.3 $\displaystyle \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \left(\frac{3}{x}\right)^k$. Find IoC and $P(x)$. (Variable in the denominator — IoC is two rays.)
2.4 $\displaystyle \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \left(\frac{x}{4}\right)^k$. Find IoC and $P(x)$. (Starts at $k=1$, so $g_0 = \tfrac{x}{4}$, not 1.)
Item 3 · Three Σ Rules (Pull-out · Split · Index Shift)
Directions: Rewrite each sum using the indicated rule(s). Do not evaluate numerically — these test whether you can manipulate symbols correctly.
4 Questions — "Σ algebra"
Rules: (I) constant pull-out, (II) sum split, (III) index shift. Q4 chains two rules.
3.1 (Rule I — pull out the constant) Rewrite $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{8} 5n$ with the constant pulled outside the Σ.
3.2 (Rule II — split the sum) Rewrite $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{12} (n^2 - 3n + 4)$ as three separate sums.
3.3 (Rule III — index shift) Rewrite $\displaystyle \sum_{n=3}^{20} n^2$ so the index starts at $n = 0$. Do not evaluate.
3.4 (Combined — pull out, then shift) Rewrite $\displaystyle \sum_{n=5}^{14} 4(n-3)^2$ by first pulling the 4 out front, then shifting the index to start at $n = 0$.
Item 4 · Bernoulli Telescoping
Directions: Set up partial fractions, then write out the partial sum and watch the middle terms cancel. Show the surviving "first + last" pieces clearly.
4 Questions — "Telescoping mechanics"
Q1 sets up the partial fractions; Q2 uses them. Q3 is a Bernoulli-style harder one. Q4 is a finite (not infinite) telescoping computation.
4.1 Find $A$ and $B$ such that $\displaystyle \frac{1}{(n+1)(n+2)} = \frac{A}{n+1} + \frac{B}{n+2}$.
4.2 Using your answer from 4.1, evaluate $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{(n+1)(n+2)}$. Show the partial sum $S_N$ and take the limit.
4.3 Evaluate $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{(4n-3)(4n+1)}$. (Bernoulli style — find $A$, $B$ first, then telescope.)
4.4 Compute the finite telescoping sum $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{4} \frac{1}{n(n+1)}$ by writing out all 4 terms (using $\frac{1}{n(n+1)} = \frac{1}{n} - \frac{1}{n+1}$) and cancelling the middle pieces. Express the answer as a single fraction.
