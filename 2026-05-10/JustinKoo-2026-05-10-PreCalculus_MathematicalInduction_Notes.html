Student: Justin Koo Date: 2026-05-10
Standard: CCSS.MATH.CONTENT.HSA-APR — Prove statements about integers using the Principle of Mathematical Induction; write rigorous base case + inductive step arguments for sums, divisibility, and inequalities.
1. Big Picture — Why Induction?

Suppose you want to prove that a formula (or property) is true for every positive integer $n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$ You cannot check infinitely many cases one by one. Mathematical Induction lets you do it in exactly two steps.

Principle of Mathematical Induction (PMI)

Let $P(n)$ be a statement about positive integers. If:

  • Base Case: $P(1)$ is true, and
  • Inductive Step: whenever $P(k)$ is true for some $k \ge 1$, then $P(k+1)$ is also true,

then $P(n)$ is true for all integers $n \ge 1$.

2. The Domino Analogy

Think of each integer $n$ as a domino standing upright. The two steps guarantee:

  • Base Case = the first domino falls.
  • Inductive Step = if any domino $k$ falls, it knocks over domino $k+1$.

Together, all dominoes fall — i.e., $P(n)$ is true for every $n \ge 1$.

3. Proof Template (Use This Every Time)
Claim: $P(n)$: [write the exact statement here] for all $n \ge 1$.
Step 1 — Base Case: Show $P(1)$ is true. Substitute $n = 1$ and verify LHS $=$ RHS (or the property holds).
Step 2 — Inductive Hypothesis: Assume $P(k)$ is true for some arbitrary but fixed $k \ge 1$.
Step 3 — Inductive Step: Using the inductive hypothesis, prove $P(k+1)$ is true. (This is the algebra-heavy part.)
Conclusion: By the Principle of Mathematical Induction, $P(n)$ is true for all integers $n \ge 1$. $\blacksquare$
Key Mindset
In the inductive step you do not assume what you are trying to prove. You assume $P(k)$ and derive $P(k+1)$ as a logical consequence. The inductive hypothesis is your "free gift" — use it!

4. Type A — Sum Formulas
The most common induction problems prove a closed-form formula for a sum. The key move in the inductive step: write out the $(k+1)$-th sum as the $k$-th sum plus the new last term, then use the inductive hypothesis to replace the $k$-th sum.
Worked Example A1 — Sum of First n Integers
Prove: $\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{n} i = 1 + 2 + 3 + \cdots + n = \dfrac{n(n+1)}{2}$ for all $n \ge 1$.

Base Case ($n = 1$): LHS $= 1$.   RHS $= \dfrac{1 \cdot 2}{2} = 1$.   $\checkmark$

Inductive Hypothesis (IH): Assume $1 + 2 + \cdots + k = \dfrac{k(k+1)}{2}$.

Inductive Step (show for $n = k+1$):

$\underbrace{1 + 2 + \cdots + k}_{\text{use IH}} + (k+1)$
$= \dfrac{k(k+1)}{2} + (k+1)$    (by IH)
$= (k+1)\!\left(\dfrac{k}{2} + 1\right)$
$= (k+1)\cdot\dfrac{k+2}{2} = \dfrac{(k+1)(k+2)}{2}$    $\checkmark$

Conclusion: The formula holds for all $n \ge 1$. $\blacksquare$

Worked Example A2 — Sum of Squares
Prove: $\displaystyle 1^2 + 2^2 + \cdots + n^2 = \dfrac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6}$ for all $n \ge 1$.

Base Case ($n = 1$): LHS $= 1$.   RHS $= \dfrac{1 \cdot 2 \cdot 3}{6} = 1$.   $\checkmark$

Inductive Hypothesis: Assume $1^2 + 2^2 + \cdots + k^2 = \dfrac{k(k+1)(2k+1)}{6}$.

Inductive Step:

$1^2 + \cdots + k^2 + (k+1)^2 = \dfrac{k(k+1)(2k+1)}{6} + (k+1)^2$
$= \dfrac{k(k+1)(2k+1) + 6(k+1)^2}{6}$
$= \dfrac{(k+1)\bigl[k(2k+1) + 6(k+1)\bigr]}{6}$
$= \dfrac{(k+1)(2k^2 + 7k + 6)}{6}$
$= \dfrac{(k+1)(k+2)(2k+3)}{6} = \dfrac{(k+1)\bigl((k+1)+1\bigr)\bigl(2(k+1)+1\bigr)}{6}$    $\checkmark$

Conclusion: The formula holds for all $n \ge 1$. $\blacksquare$

5. Type B — Geometric Series Formula (Connection to Last Week)
Last week we derived the geometric series formula by "multiplying the trick." Today we prove that same formula rigorously using induction. The algebra in the inductive step requires factoring out $(r - 1)$.
Worked Example B — Geometric Series
Prove: $\displaystyle 1 + r + r^2 + \cdots + r^{n-1} = \dfrac{r^n - 1}{r - 1}$ for all $n \ge 1$, $r \ne 1$.

Base Case ($n = 1$): LHS $= r^0 = 1$.   RHS $= \dfrac{r - 1}{r - 1} = 1$.   $\checkmark$

Inductive Hypothesis: Assume $1 + r + \cdots + r^{k-1} = \dfrac{r^k - 1}{r - 1}$.

Inductive Step (prove for $n = k+1$):

$1 + r + \cdots + r^{k-1} + r^k$
$= \dfrac{r^k - 1}{r - 1} + r^k$    (by IH)
$= \dfrac{r^k - 1 + r^k(r-1)}{r - 1}$
$= \dfrac{r^k - 1 + r^{k+1} - r^k}{r - 1}$
$= \dfrac{r^{k+1} - 1}{r - 1}$    $\checkmark$

Conclusion: The formula holds for all $n \ge 1$. $\blacksquare$

Why Does This Matter?

The geometric series formula $\displaystyle\sum_{i=0}^{n-1} r^i = \dfrac{r^n - 1}{r-1}$ is the same one we used last week for partial sums of geometric sequences. Now we know it is not just a pattern — it is a theorem with a rigorous proof.

For $|r| < 1$, as $n \to \infty$, $r^n \to 0$, giving the infinite sum: $\displaystyle\sum_{i=0}^{\infty} r^i = \dfrac{1}{1-r}$. Induction proves the finite case; the limit gives the infinite case.

6. Type C — Divisibility Proofs
Divisibility proofs have a slightly different structure. Instead of showing two sums are equal, you show that an expression is always a multiple of some fixed integer $d$. The key move: write $f(k+1)$ in terms of $f(k)$ by expanding and regrouping.
Divisibility Notation

"$d \mid f(n)$" means "$d$ divides $f(n)$" — i.e., $f(n) = d \cdot m$ for some integer $m$.

IH form for divisibility: Assume $f(k) = d \cdot m$ for some integer $m$. Then show $f(k+1) = d \cdot (\text{integer})$.

Worked Example C — Divisibility by 3
Prove: $3 \mid (n^3 - n)$ for all $n \ge 1$.

Base Case ($n = 1$): $1^3 - 1 = 0 = 3 \cdot 0$.   Divisible by 3.   $\checkmark$

Inductive Hypothesis: Assume $k^3 - k = 3m$ for some integer $m$.

Inductive Step (show $3 \mid (k+1)^3 - (k+1)$):

$(k+1)^3 - (k+1)$
$= k^3 + 3k^2 + 3k + 1 - k - 1$    (expand)
$= \underbrace{(k^3 - k)}_{\text{use IH}} + 3k^2 + 3k$
$= 3m + 3(k^2 + k)$    (by IH)
$= 3\bigl(m + k^2 + k\bigr)$, divisible by 3.   $\checkmark$

Conclusion: $3 \mid (n^3 - n)$ for all $n \ge 1$. $\blacksquare$

Divisibility Proof Strategy
  1. Expand $f(k+1)$ fully.
  2. Identify and isolate $f(k)$ inside the expansion.
  3. Replace $f(k)$ using the inductive hypothesis.
  4. Factor out $d$ from the entire expression.

7. Type D — Inequality Proofs
Inequality induction uses the same two-step framework, but the inductive step involves a chain of inequalities rather than equalities. Justify every inequality sign — each one must follow from either the IH or basic algebra.
Worked Example D1 — Exponential vs. Linear ($2^n > n$)
Prove: $2^n > n$ for all $n \ge 1$.

Base Case ($n = 1$): $2^1 = 2 > 1$.   $\checkmark$

Inductive Hypothesis: Assume $2^k > k$ for some $k \ge 1$.

Inductive Step (show $2^{k+1} > k+1$):

$2^{k+1} = 2 \cdot 2^k > 2k$    (by IH, multiply both sides by 2)
$2k = k + k \ge k + 1$    (since $k \ge 1$)
$\therefore \; 2^{k+1} > k + 1$    $\checkmark$

Conclusion: $2^n > n$ for all $n \ge 1$. $\blacksquare$

Worked Example D2 — Exponential vs. Quadratic ($2^n \ge n^2$, $n \ge 4$)
Prove: $2^n \ge n^2$ for all integers $n \ge 4$.
(Note: fails for $n = 1,2,3$ — the base case starts at $n = 4$.)

Base Case ($n = 4$): $2^4 = 16$ and $4^2 = 16$.   $16 \ge 16$.   $\checkmark$

Inductive Hypothesis: Assume $2^k \ge k^2$ for some $k \ge 4$.

Inductive Step (show $2^{k+1} \ge (k+1)^2$):

$2^{k+1} = 2 \cdot 2^k \ge 2k^2$    (by IH)
It suffices to show $2k^2 \ge (k+1)^2 = k^2 + 2k + 1$,
i.e., $k^2 - 2k - 1 \ge 0$, i.e., $(k-1)^2 \ge 2$.
Since $k \ge 4$: $(k-1) \ge 3 > \sqrt{2}$, so $(k-1)^2 \ge 9 > 2$.   $\checkmark$
Therefore $2^{k+1} \ge 2k^2 \ge (k+1)^2$.   $\checkmark$

Conclusion: $2^n \ge n^2$ for all $n \ge 4$. $\blacksquare$

8. Summary — Four Types of Induction
Type What to Prove Key Move in Inductive Step
A — Sum Formula $\sum_{i=1}^n f(i) = \text{formula}$ Split off last term; use IH to replace the partial sum.
B — Geometric Series $\sum r^i = \frac{r^n-1}{r-1}$ Add $r^k$ to both sides; factor out $(r-1)$ numerator.
C — Divisibility $d \mid f(n)$ Expand $f(k+1)$; isolate $f(k)$; factor out $d$.
D — Inequality $f(n) \ge g(n)$ (or $>$) Chain inequalities; justify each $\ge$ from IH or $k \ge$ base.
