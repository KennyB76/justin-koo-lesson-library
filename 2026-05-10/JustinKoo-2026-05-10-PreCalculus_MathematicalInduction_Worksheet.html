Proof by Mathematical Induction — Two-Step Framework

Step 1 — Base Case: Show the statement $P(n)$ is true for $n = 1$ (or the first relevant integer).

Step 2 — Inductive Step: Assume $P(k)$ is true (inductive hypothesis). Then prove $P(k+1)$ is true.

Conclusion: By the Principle of Mathematical Induction, $P(n)$ is true for all $n \ge 1$.

Problem 1 — Sum of First n Positive Integers
Difficulty: Introductory. Follow the template below. Write each step clearly.
Worked Example — Sum of First n Odd Numbers
Prove by induction: $\displaystyle 1 + 3 + 5 + \cdots + (2n-1) = n^2$ for all $n \ge 1$.

Base Case ($n = 1$): LHS $= 1$.   RHS $= 1^2 = 1$.   $\checkmark$

Inductive Hypothesis: Assume $1 + 3 + \cdots + (2k-1) = k^2$ for some $k \ge 1$.

Inductive Step (prove for $n = k+1$):

$1 + 3 + \cdots + (2k-1) + (2(k+1)-1)$
$= k^2 + (2k+1)$    (by inductive hypothesis)
$= (k+1)^2$    (factor)

Conclusion: The statement holds for all $n \ge 1$. $\blacksquare$

Worked Example — Sum of First n Even Numbers
Prove by induction: $\displaystyle 2 + 4 + 6 + \cdots + 2n = n(n+1)$ for all $n \ge 1$.

Base Case ($n=1$): LHS $= 2$.   RHS $= 1 \cdot 2 = 2$.   $\checkmark$

Inductive Hypothesis: Assume $2 + 4 + \cdots + 2k = k(k+1)$.

Inductive Step:

$2 + 4 + \cdots + 2k + 2(k+1)$
$= k(k+1) + 2(k+1)$    (by inductive hypothesis)
$= (k+1)(k + 2)$    (factor out $(k+1)$)
$= (k+1)\bigl((k+1)+1\bigr)$    $\checkmark$

Conclusion: $P(n)$ holds for all $n \ge 1$. $\blacksquare$

Worked Example — Powers of 2
Prove by induction: $\displaystyle 1 + 2 + 4 + \cdots + 2^{n-1} = 2^n - 1$ for all $n \ge 1$.

Base Case ($n=1$): LHS $= 2^0 = 1$.   RHS $= 2^1 - 1 = 1$.   $\checkmark$

Inductive Hypothesis: Assume $1 + 2 + \cdots + 2^{k-1} = 2^k - 1$.

Inductive Step:

$1 + 2 + \cdots + 2^{k-1} + 2^k$
$= (2^k - 1) + 2^k$    (by inductive hypothesis)
$= 2 \cdot 2^k - 1 = 2^{k+1} - 1$    $\checkmark$

Conclusion: $P(n)$ holds for all $n \ge 1$. $\blacksquare$

Problem 4 — Divisibility Proof
Difficulty: Medium-Hard. Divisibility proofs use a different structure than sum proofs. The key move is writing $f(k+1)$ in terms of $f(k)$.
Worked Example — Divisibility by 3
Prove by induction: $n^3 - n$ is divisible by 3 for all $n \ge 1$.

Base Case ($n=1$): $1^3 - 1 = 0 = 3 \cdot 0$.   Divisible by 3.   $\checkmark$

Inductive Hypothesis: Assume $k^3 - k = 3m$ for some integer $m$.

Inductive Step:

$(k+1)^3 - (k+1)$
$= k^3 + 3k^2 + 3k + 1 - k - 1$
$= (k^3 - k) + 3k^2 + 3k$
$= 3m + 3(k^2 + k)$    (by inductive hypothesis)
$= 3(m + k^2 + k)$, which is divisible by 3.   $\checkmark$

Conclusion: $3 \mid (n^3 - n)$ for all $n \ge 1$. $\blacksquare$

Problem 5 — Inequality Proof
Difficulty: Hard. Inequality induction requires careful reasoning about the direction of the inequality at each step. Justify every inequality sign.
Worked Example — Exponential vs. Linear
Prove by induction: $2^n > n$ for all $n \ge 1$.

Base Case ($n=1$): $2^1 = 2 > 1$.   $\checkmark$

Inductive Hypothesis: Assume $2^k > k$ for some $k \ge 1$.

Inductive Step:

$2^{k+1} = 2 \cdot 2^k > 2k$    (by inductive hypothesis, multiply both sides by 2)
$2k = k + k \ge k + 1$    (since $k \ge 1$)
Therefore $2^{k+1} > k + 1$.   $\checkmark$

Conclusion: $2^n > n$ for all $n \ge 1$. $\blacksquare$
