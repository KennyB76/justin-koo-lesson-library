Sums and the Dimension Jump

Student: Justin Koo Date: May 10, 2026
Standard: CCSS.MATH.CONTENT.HSF-BF.A.1 — Build a function that models a relationship between two quantities; recognize and apply patterns in power sums and their connection to integration.   Big Idea: Summing $d$-dimensional terms always produces a $(d+1)$-dimensional result — the same rule that drives the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus.
Formulas to Know
$$\sum_{k=1}^{n} k = \frac{n(n+1)}{2} \qquad \sum_{k=1}^{n} k^2 = \frac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6} \qquad \sum_{k=1}^{n} k^3 = \left(\frac{n(n+1)}{2}\right)^{\!2}$$

Dimension-Jump Rule: $\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{n} k^d \;\approx\; \dfrac{n^{\,d+1}}{d+1}$  as $n\to\infty$.   Linearity: $\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{n}(af(k)+bg(k)) = a\sum f(k) + b\sum g(k)$.

Part B — Geometric & Conceptual Reasoning
Directions: These problems ask you to connect the formulas to geometry and the dimension-jump idea. Write clearly in complete sentences where indicated.
4. A staircase is built so that step $k$ has width $k$ and height 1 (for $k = 1, 2, \ldots, n$). What is the total area of the staircase when $n = 12$? Which sum formula applies, and why?

Part D — Challenge & Reflection
Directions: Problem 8 is a multi-step challenge; Problem 9 is a written reflection. Both require complete sentences for full credit.
8. (Challenge)   A physicist models the total mass of a system as $M = \displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{n} k^3$ grams.
(a) Compute $M$ exactly for $n = 8$ using the sum-of-cubes formula.
(b) Compute $\left(\dfrac{n(n+1)}{2}\right)^2$ for $n=8$ and compare it to your answer in (a). What do you notice? Why does this make sense?
9. (Reflection)   The Dimension-Jump Rule states $\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{n} k^d \approx \dfrac{n^{\,d+1}}{d+1}$. The Power Rule for integrals states $\displaystyle\int_0^n x^d\, dx = \dfrac{n^{\,d+1}}{d+1}$. Is this a coincidence? In 2–3 sentences, explain what you think is happening.
