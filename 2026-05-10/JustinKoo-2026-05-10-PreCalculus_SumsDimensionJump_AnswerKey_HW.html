Part A — Direct Application of Sum Formulas
Problem 1
Evaluate $\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{20} k$.
sum of integers formula application
Use $\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{n} k = \dfrac{n(n+1)}{2}$ with $n = 20$:
$$\frac{20 \cdot 21}{2} = \frac{420}{2} = 210$$
210
Problem 2
Evaluate $\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{15} k^2$.
sum of squares formula application
Use $\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{n} k^2 = \dfrac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6}$ with $n = 15$:
$$\frac{15 \cdot 16 \cdot 31}{6} = \frac{7440}{6} = 1240$$
1240
Problem 3
Evaluate $\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{10} k^3$.
sum of cubes formula application
Use $\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{n} k^3 = \left(\dfrac{n(n+1)}{2}\right)^2$ with $n = 10$:
$$\left(\frac{10 \cdot 11}{2}\right)^2 = (55)^2 = 3025$$
3025
Part B — Geometric & Conceptual Reasoning
Problem 4
A staircase with step widths $1, 2, \ldots, n$ (height 1 each). Total area when $n=12$?
sum of integers geometric interpretation
Each step $k$ has area $= k \times 1 = k$. Total area $= \displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{12} k$.
The sum-of-integers formula applies because each term is $k$ (1-dimensional).
$$\sum_{k=1}^{12} k = \frac{12 \cdot 13}{2} = \frac{156}{2} = 78$$
Dimension-jump check: summing 1D terms gives a 2D area result — consistent with the rule.
Total area = 78 square units   (sum-of-integers formula)
Problem 5
Is $\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{100} k^2$ closest to $\dfrac{100^2}{3}$, $\dfrac{100^3}{3}$, or $\dfrac{100^4}{3}$?
dimension-jump rule estimation
Answer: $\dfrac{100^3}{3}$.
By the Dimension-Jump Rule, $\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{n} k^d \approx \dfrac{n^{d+1}}{d+1}$.
Here $d = 2$, so the sum $\approx \dfrac{n^{3}}{3} = \dfrac{100^3}{3} \approx 333{,}333$.
Check: exact value $= \dfrac{100 \cdot 101 \cdot 201}{6} = 338{,}350$ — very close to $\dfrac{100^3}{3}$, not to $\dfrac{100^2}{3} \approx 3{,}333$ or $\dfrac{100^4}{3} \approx 33.3$ million.
$\dfrac{100^3}{3} \approx 333{,}333$   (exact: 338,350)
Problem 6
Complete: $\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{n} k^4 \approx \underline{\hspace{60pt}}$ as $n\to\infty$. What dimension?
dimension-jump rule generalization
Apply the rule with $d = 4$:   $\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{n} k^4 \approx \dfrac{n^{5}}{5}$.
The leading term is $n^5$, so the result lives in 5 dimensions.
Pattern: summing $k^4$ (4D terms) produces a 5D result — the dimension always jumps by 1.
$\dfrac{n^5}{5}$  —  5-dimensional
Part C — Algebra with Sum Linearity
Problem 7
Compute $\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{n} (3k^2 + 2k - 1)$. Leave in terms of $n$.
linearity of summation algebraic simplification
Split using linearity: $$\sum_{k=1}^{n}(3k^2 + 2k - 1) = 3\sum_{k=1}^{n}k^2 \;+\; 2\sum_{k=1}^{n}k \;-\; \sum_{k=1}^{n}1$$
Apply each formula: $$= 3\cdot\frac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6} \;+\; 2\cdot\frac{n(n+1)}{2} \;-\; n$$
Simplify each term: $$= \frac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{2} \;+\; n(n+1) \;-\; n$$
Factor out $n$ and expand: $$= n\left[\frac{(n+1)(2n+1)}{2} + (n+1) - 1\right]$$
Combine over common denominator 2: $$= n\cdot\frac{(n+1)(2n+1) + 2(n+1) - 2}{2} = n\cdot\frac{(n+1)(2n+3) - 2}{2}$$
Expand $(n+1)(2n+3) = 2n^2 + 5n + 3$, so numerator $= 2n^2 + 5n + 1$: $$\boxed{= \frac{n(2n^2+5n+1)}{2}}$$
Verify with $n=2$:   $(3 \cdot 4 + 2 \cdot 2 - 1) + (3 \cdot 1 + 2 \cdot 1 - 1) = 15 + 4 = 19$.   Formula: $\frac{2(8+10+1)}{2} = 19$ ✓
$\dfrac{n(2n^2 + 5n + 1)}{2}$
Part D — Challenge & Reflection
Problem 8 (Challenge)
$M = \displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{n} k^3$. (a) Compute $M$ for $n=8$. (b) Compare to $\left(\frac{n(n+1)}{2}\right)^2$ for $n=8$.
sum of cubes identity verification
(a) Apply $\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{n} k^3 = \left(\dfrac{n(n+1)}{2}\right)^2$ with $n=8$: $$M = \left(\frac{8 \cdot 9}{2}\right)^2 = (36)^2 = 1296$$
(b) $\left(\dfrac{n(n+1)}{2}\right)^2 = (36)^2 = 1296$ for $n=8$.
The two values are identical — this is not a coincidence. The formula is $\left(\frac{n(n+1)}{2}\right)^2$; both expressions are the same formula, just written differently. The sum of cubes $1^3 + 2^3 + \cdots + n^3$ always equals the square of the triangular number $\frac{n(n+1)}{2}$.
(a) $M = 1296$  |  (b) Same value — the formula $\sum k^3 = \left(\frac{n(n+1)}{2}\right)^2$ is exact, not an approximation
Problem 9 (Reflection)
Dimension-Jump Rule: $\sum k^d \approx n^{d+1}/(d+1)$. Power Rule: $\int_0^n x^d\,dx = n^{d+1}/(d+1)$. Coincidence?
Riemann sums calculus preview
It is not a coincidence.
The sum $\sum_{k=1}^{n} k^d$ can be written as $n^{d+1}\sum_{k=1}^{n}\left(\frac{k}{n}\right)^d \cdot \frac{1}{n}$, which is a Riemann sum approximating $\int_0^1 x^d\,dx \cdot n^{d+1} = \dfrac{n^{d+1}}{d+1}$.
In other words, the dimension-jump rule is the Power Rule for integration in disguise: the act of summing infinitely many thin slices is exactly what an integral does, and the $+1$ in the exponent comes from accumulating one extra dimension each time you "sum up" a quantity.
Not a coincidence — $\sum k^d$ is a Riemann sum for $\int_0^n x^d\,dx$; both equal $n^{d+1}/(d+1)$
Teacher Note
This is the conceptual bridge between discrete summation (Algebra/Pre-Calc) and the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus. Students who see this clearly are primed for AP Calculus integration from day one.
