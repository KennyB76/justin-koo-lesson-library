Mathematical Induction: Inequality Proofs

Student: Justin Koo Date: 2026-05-10
Topics: Proving inequalities by induction — the Wider Scope Tactic — Bernoulli's Inequality — $2^n > n^2$ for $n \ge 5$.
1. Quick Recap — The Two-Step Structure

Every induction proof has the same skeleton, whether you are proving a sum formula, a divisibility claim, or an inequality:

Base Case: Verify the statement $P(n_0)$ at the smallest value $n_0$ directly.
Inductive Step: Assume $P(k)$ (the Inductive Hypothesis, IH). Derive $P(k+1)$ as a logical consequence.

For inequality proofs the inductive step is a chain of inequalities. Each link in the chain must be justified — either by the IH, basic algebra, or the fact that $k \ge n_0$.

2. Why Inequality Steps Are Harder

With sum formulas you replace the partial sum with the closed form and check equality. With inequalities you cannot simply substitute — you must build a bridge from $f(k)$ to $f(k+1)$ using valid inequality moves:

  • If $A \ge B$ and $C \ge 0$, then $A + C \ge B + C$ and $AC \ge BC$.
  • If $A \ge B \ge C$, then $A \ge C$ (transitivity).
  • You may replace an expression with something larger when you need an upper bound, or something smaller when you need a lower bound — as long as the chain still reaches the target.
Key Mindset for Inequality Proofs
You do not need to go directly from $f(k)$ to $f(k+1)$. It is perfectly valid to use a detour through a larger quantity, as long as you can connect the endpoints of the chain. This idea is formalized in the next section.

3. The Wider Scope Tactic
Kenny's Key Technique
Wrap Both Sides in a Wider Scope

Suppose you need to prove $A(k+1)$ in the inductive step, but going from $A(k)$ to $A(k+1)$ directly is messy or impossible with the tools available.

Find a stronger bound $B(k+1)$ where $B \ge A$, and prove $B(k+1)$ instead.

Since $B(k+1) \ge A(k+1)$, proving $B(k+1)$ is enough. The "wider scope" makes the inductive step easier to close.

In practice: after applying the IH, you often get an expression like $2k^2$ where you need $(k+1)^2$. Showing $2k^2 \ge (k+1)^2$ is the "wider scope" bridge.

4. Worked Example 1 — Bernoulli's Inequality
Bernoulli's Inequality: $(1+x)^n \ge 1 + nx$ for $x > -1$, $n \ge 1$
Prove: For any real $x > -1$ and all integers $n \ge 1$, $$\displaystyle (1+x)^n \;\ge\; 1 + nx.$$

Base Case ($n = 1$):   $(1+x)^1 = 1 + x = 1 + 1 \cdot x$.   $\checkmark$

Inductive Hypothesis:   Assume $(1+x)^k \ge 1 + kx$ for some fixed $k \ge 1$.

Inductive Step (show $(1+x)^{k+1} \ge 1 + (k+1)x$):

$(1+x)^{k+1} = (1+x)^k \cdot (1+x)$

$\ge (1 + kx)(1+x)$    [by IH; valid since $(1+x) \ge 0$ when $x > -1$]

$= 1 + kx + x + kx^2$

$= 1 + (k+1)x + kx^2$

$\ge 1 + (k+1)x$    [since $kx^2 \ge 0$ for all real $x$]

Conclusion: $(1+x)^n \ge 1 + nx$ for all $n \ge 1$ and $x > -1$. $\blacksquare$

Where is the Wider Scope? After multiplying by $(1+x)$ we obtained $1 + (k+1)x + kx^2$. We did not need the exact value of $kx^2$; we only needed to know $kx^2 \ge 0$ to drop it. Dropping the non-negative term is the "wider scope" move — we proved something slightly larger and used that as the lower bound.

5. Worked Example 2 — Exponential vs. Quadratic ($2^n > n^2$)
Prove: $2^n > n^2$ for all integers $n \ge 5$
Note: the statement fails for $n = 1, 2, 3, 4$. Check: $2^4 = 16 = 4^2$, so the base case starts at $n = 5$.

Base Case ($n = 5$):   $2^5 = 32$ and $5^2 = 25$.   $32 > 25$.   $\checkmark$

Inductive Hypothesis:   Assume $2^k > k^2$ for some $k \ge 5$.

Inductive Step (show $2^{k+1} > (k+1)^2$):

$2^{k+1} = 2 \cdot 2^k > 2k^2$    [by IH, multiplying both sides by 2]

It now suffices to show: $$2k^2 \;\ge\; (k+1)^2 = k^2 + 2k + 1$$ i.e., $k^2 - 2k - 1 \ge 0$,   i.e., $(k-1)^2 \ge 2$.

Since $k \ge 5$:   $k - 1 \ge 4 > \sqrt{2}$,   so $(k-1)^2 \ge 16 > 2$.   $\checkmark$

Therefore: $\;2^{k+1} > 2k^2 \ge (k+1)^2$.   $\checkmark$

Conclusion: $2^n > n^2$ for all $n \ge 5$. $\blacksquare$

Where is the Wider Scope? We needed $(k+1)^2$, but after using the IH we only had $2k^2$. Instead of trying to go directly to $(k+1)^2$, we asked: "Is $2k^2$ big enough to cover $(k+1)^2$?" That sub-problem ($2k^2 \ge (k+1)^2$ for $k \ge 5$) is the "wider scope" bridge.

Checklist for Every Inequality Induction Proof
  • State the IH precisely with the correct direction ($\ge$ or $>$).
  • Write a chain: start from $f(k+1)$, introduce the IH, then close with the target.
  • Justify every step: "by IH", "since $k \ge n_0$", "since $t^2 \ge 0$", etc.
  • If you need a bridge, ask: "What weaker thing can I prove that still implies my target?" — that is the Wider Scope Tactic.

6. Two More Inequalities Worth Knowing
These appear often on tests and competitions. Both use the Wider Scope Tactic in their proofs. The proofs are outlined below — a good exercise is to fill in the justifications yourself.
Fact 1:   $n^2 \ge 2n + 1$   for $n \ge 3$

Proof sketch: Base case $n = 3$: $9 \ge 7$. $\checkmark$   IH: $k^2 \ge 2k + 1$. Then $(k+1)^2 = k^2 + 2k + 1 \ge (2k+1) + 2k + 1 = 4k + 2 \ge 2(k+1) + 1$ (since $4k + 2 \ge 2k + 3$ iff $2k \ge 1$, true for $k \ge 1$). $\blacksquare$

Fact 2:   $n! > 2^n$   for $n \ge 4$

Proof sketch: Base case $n = 4$: $24 > 16$. $\checkmark$   IH: $k! > 2^k$ for some $k \ge 4$. Then $(k+1)! = (k+1) \cdot k! > (k+1) \cdot 2^k \ge 2 \cdot 2^k = 2^{k+1}$ (since $k+1 \ge 5 > 2$ for $k \ge 4$). $\blacksquare$

Wider scope: replacing $(k+1)$ with just $2$ gives a weaker lower bound, but it is enough to close the chain.

7. Summary — Inequality Induction at a Glance
Inequality Base Case Starts Key Bridge (Wider Scope)
$(1+x)^n \ge 1+nx$ $n = 1$ Drop $kx^2 \ge 0$ to simplify the product
$2^n > n^2$ $n = 5$ Show $2k^2 \ge (k+1)^2$ as a sub-problem
$n^2 \ge 2n+1$ $n = 3$ Use IH to replace $k^2$; simplify the bound
$n! > 2^n$ $n = 4$ Replace $(k+1)$ with $2$ to get $2^{k+1}$ directly
Always Label Your Reasoning
In a proof, every inequality line must have a reason in brackets: [by IH], [since $k \ge 5$], [since $t^2 \ge 0$], etc. Writing "obviously" or leaving it blank is not accepted in a formal proof.
