1. Problem 1 — Tile a 6-Unit Walkway

In how many different ways can you tile a 6-unit walkway with 1-unit squares and 2-unit rectangles? Order matters.

Method A — Case analysis (count by number of 2-unit tiles)

Suppose you use $k$ rectangles (length 2) and $(6 - 2k)$ squares (length 1). Total tiles: $6 - 2k + k = 6 - k$.

The number of arrangements is the number of ways to choose which positions in the sequence of $(6-k)$ tiles are rectangles: $\dbinom{6 - k}{k}$.

$k$ (# rect)# sqTotal tilesArrangement count $\binom{6-k}{k}$
066$\binom{6}{0} = 1$
145$\binom{5}{1} = 5$
224$\binom{4}{2} = 6$
303$\binom{3}{3} = 1$
Total$1+5+6+1 = \mathbf{13}$

Method B — Recursion (cleaner; we'll use this everywhere)

Let $L_n$ = number of tilings of an $n$-strip. Look at the very first tile placed at position 1:

CASE 1 First tile is a 1-unit square. Remaining strip has length $n-1$ → $L_{n-1}$ tilings.
CASE 2 First tile is a 2-unit rectangle. Remaining strip has length $n-2$ → $L_{n-2}$ tilings.
RECURRENCE
$L_n = L_{n-1} + L_{n-2}$,   $L_1 = 1$,   $L_2 = 2$    (and $L_0 = 1$ by convention)

Build up:

The Fibonacci connection: If you compare $L_n$ to the standard Fibonacci $F_n$ ($F_1 = F_2 = 1, F_3 = 2, \ldots$), you'll see $L_n = F_{n+1}$. The Lego problem is Fibonacci — just shifted by one index.

3. Problem 3 — General Method for an $n$-Strip

Derive a general method for the number of tilings of an $n$-strip.

We did the work in Problem 1 already. Restating cleanly as a recursive definition (the format Mr. Jones is asking for):

RECURSIVE DEFINITION

I. Recurrence relation:   $L_n = L_{n-1} + L_{n-2}$ for all $n \ge 3$

II. Initial values:   $L_1 = 1, \;\; L_2 = 2$
The "look at the first tile" argument (write this down on the test):
Every valid tiling of an $n$-strip starts with either a square (leaving an $(n-1)$-strip to tile in $L_{n-1}$ ways) or a rectangle (leaving an $(n-2)$-strip to tile in $L_{n-2}$ ways). These cases don't overlap and cover everything, so $L_n = L_{n-1} + L_{n-2}$.

4. Problem 4 — Tiling Argument: $L_{10} = L_4 L_6 + L_3 L_5$

Use a tiling argument to explain why $L_{10} = L_4 L_6 + L_3 L_5$.

The "cut" idea

Look at any tiling of a 10-strip. Draw a vertical cut between unit 4 and unit 5. Exactly one of two things must be true:

CASE A — No 2-tile straddles the cut.

The cut splits the tiling into two independent pieces:

121
2121 left = 4-strip · right = 6-strip

Left piece (units 1-4): any tiling of a 4-strip → $L_4$ choices.
Right piece (units 5-10): any tiling of a 6-strip → $L_6$ choices.

Case A total = $L_4 \cdot L_6$

CASE B — A 2-tile covers units 4 and 5.

That single rectangle straddles the cut. The remaining tiling splits as:

12 2 1121 left = 3-strip · straddler · right = 5-strip

Left piece (units 1-3): $L_3$ choices.
Right piece (units 6-10): $L_5$ choices.
The red 2-tile straddling 4-5 is fixed (no choice involved).

Case B total = $L_3 \cdot L_5$

Cases A and B are mutually exclusive (either a 2-tile covers 4-5 or it doesn't) and exhaustive (every tiling falls into one). So:

$L_{10} = L_4 \cdot L_6 + L_3 \cdot L_5 \quad \blacksquare$

Numerical sanity check

$L_4 = 5, \;\; L_6 = 13, \;\; L_3 = 3, \;\; L_5 = 8$.
$L_4 \cdot L_6 + L_3 \cdot L_5 = 5(13) + 3(8) = 65 + 24 = 89$.
And $L_{10} = 89$ from the table above. ✓

5. Problem 5 — Tiling Argument: $L_{2n} = L_n^2 + L_{n-1}^2$

Use a tiling argument to explain why $L_{2n} = L_n^2 + L_{n-1}^2$ for $n \ge 1$.

This is the same idea as Problem 4, but with the cut placed at the midpoint of a $2n$-strip.

Tile a $2n$-strip and draw a cut between unit $n$ and unit $n+1$.

CASE A — No 2-tile straddles the midpoint cut.

Splits cleanly into two $n$-strips.
Left: $L_n$ choices. Right: $L_n$ choices.

Case A total = $L_n \cdot L_n = L_n^2$

CASE B — A 2-tile covers units $n$ and $n+1$.

That rectangle is forced. What's left to tile: units 1 through $n-1$, and units $n+2$ through $2n$ — both are $(n-1)$-strips.
Left: $L_{n-1}$ choices. Right: $L_{n-1}$ choices.

Case B total = $L_{n-1} \cdot L_{n-1} = L_{n-1}^2$

$L_{2n} = L_n^2 + L_{n-1}^2 \quad \blacksquare$

Sanity check with $n = 5$ (so $2n = 10$):

$L_5 = 8, \;\; L_4 = 5$.
$L_5^2 + L_4^2 = 64 + 25 = 89$.
$L_{10} = 89$. ✓
The general pattern (worth memorizing): for any cut between position $m$ and $m+1$ in an $(m+k)$-strip, $$L_{m+k} = L_m L_k + L_{m-1} L_{k-1}.$$ Problem 4 is $m=4, k=6$. Problem 5 is $m=k=n$. We'll use the general version in Problem 6.

6. Problem 6 [Challenge] — Formula for $L_{3n}$

Create a formula for computing $L_{3n}$ (for $n \ge 2$) by thinking about what happens at units $n$ and $2n$. Your formula should involve $L_n$, $L_{n-1}$, and $L_{n-2}$.

Strategy: cut at unit $2n$, then cut what's left at unit $n$

Apply the general cut formula $\;L_{m+k} = L_m L_k + L_{m-1} L_{k-1}\;$ twice.

CUT 1 Split $L_{3n}$ at the boundary between unit $2n$ and unit $2n+1$ (so $m = 2n$, $k = n$): $$L_{3n} = L_{2n}\,L_n + L_{2n-1}\,L_{n-1} \tag{$\ast$}$$
CUT 2a We already know from Problem 5: $\;L_{2n} = L_n^2 + L_{n-1}^2$.
CUT 2b Apply the general cut formula to $L_{2n-1} = L_{n + (n-1)}$ with $m = n$, $k = n-1$: $$L_{2n-1} = L_n L_{n-1} + L_{n-1} L_{n-2} = L_{n-1}\bigl(L_n + L_{n-2}\bigr).$$
SUBSTITUTE Plug both into ($\ast$):
$L_{3n} = (L_n^2 + L_{n-1}^2)\, L_n \;+\; L_{n-1}(L_n + L_{n-2})\, L_{n-1}$

$\quad\;\; = L_n^3 + L_n L_{n-1}^2 \;+\; L_{n-1}^2 L_n + L_{n-1}^2 L_{n-2}$

$\quad\;\; = L_n^3 + 2\,L_n L_{n-1}^2 + L_{n-1}^2 L_{n-2}$
ANSWER
$L_{3n} = L_n^3 + 2\,L_n L_{n-1}^2 + L_{n-1}^2 L_{n-2}$

Sanity check with $n = 2$ (so $3n = 6$):

$L_2 = 2, \;\; L_1 = 1, \;\; L_0 = 1$.
$L_2^3 + 2 L_2 L_1^2 + L_1^2 L_0 = 8 + 2(2)(1) + (1)(1) = 8 + 4 + 1 = 13$.
$L_6 = 13$. ✓

Sanity check with $n = 3$ (so $3n = 9$):

$L_3 = 3, \;\; L_2 = 2, \;\; L_1 = 1$.
$L_3^3 + 2 L_3 L_2^2 + L_2^2 L_1 = 27 + 2(3)(4) + (4)(1) = 27 + 24 + 4 = 55$.
$L_9 = 55$. ✓
$L_{3n} = L_n^3 + 2 L_n L_{n-1}^2 + L_{n-1}^2 L_{n-2}$

7. Pre-Calc Connection — Characteristic Equation & Closed Form

🔑 Lego is a degree-2 homogeneous recurrence — your test's bread and butter.

Towers of Hanoi gave you $T(n) = 2T(n-1) + 1$ — first-order, non-homogeneous. Lego gives you

$L_n = L_{n-1} + L_{n-2}$ — second-order, homogeneous.

The closed form drops out of the characteristic equation.

7.1 Five-step characteristic-equation method (your study notes 5단계)

STEP 1 Identify the recurrence: $L_n = a_1 L_{n-1} + a_2 L_{n-2}$ with $a_1 = 1$, $a_2 = 1$.
STEP 2 Write the characteristic equation $\;x^2 - a_1 x - a_2 = 0$: $$x^2 - x - 1 = 0$$ Solve: $\;x = \dfrac{1 \pm \sqrt{5}}{2}$. Call the roots $$\varphi = \frac{1+\sqrt{5}}{2} \approx 1.618 \quad\text{(the golden ratio)}, \qquad \psi = \frac{1-\sqrt{5}}{2} \approx -0.618.$$
STEP 3 Two distinct roots → general solution $$L_n = C_1 \varphi^n + C_2 \psi^n.$$
STEP 4 Use $L_0 = 1$ and $L_1 = 1$:
$C_1 + C_2 = 1$
$C_1 \varphi + C_2 \psi = 1$
Solve: $\;C_1 = \dfrac{\varphi}{\sqrt{5}}, \;\; C_2 = -\dfrac{\psi}{\sqrt{5}}\;$  (uses $\varphi - \psi = \sqrt{5}$).
STEP 5 Closed form (Binet-style): $$\boxed{\;L_n = \dfrac{\varphi^{n+1} - \psi^{n+1}}{\sqrt{5}}\;}$$ Equivalently, $L_n = F_{n+1}$ where $F_n$ is the standard Fibonacci sequence.

7.2 Lego vs Hanoi — the same machinery

ProblemRecurrenceOrderToolClosed form
Towers of Hanoi $T_n = 2T_{n-1} + 1$ 1st, +1 (non-hom.) shift trick $T_n = 2^n - 1$
Adjacent-only Hanoi $A_n = 3A_{n-1} + 2$ 1st, +2 (non-hom.) shift trick $A_n = 3^n - 1$
Lego (Fibonacci) $L_n = L_{n-1} + L_{n-2}$ 2nd, hom. characteristic equation ⭐ $L_n = (\varphi^{n+1} - \psi^{n+1})/\sqrt{5}$

7.3 Two patterns to recognize on test day

PATTERN 1 If the recurrence is degree 1 (only one previous term) plus a constant — use the shift trick (Hanoi-style).
PATTERN 2 If the recurrence is degree 2 (two previous terms) and homogeneous — use the characteristic equation (Lego/Fibonacci-style).
⚠️ Trap to remember: when the characteristic equation has a double root $r$, the form is $(C_1 + C_2 n)\,r^n$ — not $C_1 r^n + C_2 r^n$. The extra factor of $n$ catches a lot of students.

💡 One-line takeaway

Lego, Hanoi, walkways, dominoes — they all reduce to the same skeleton: look at the last (or first, or middle) thing placed, count by mutually-exclusive cases, and you've got a recurrence. Closed form is whichever method (shift trick vs. characteristic equation) the recurrence's shape calls for.
