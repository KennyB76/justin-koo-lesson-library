Part A · Arithmetic Sequences
Problem 1 — Given $t_1 = 12, t_2 = 18$, find $t_{20}$.
Step 1. $d = t_2 - t_1 = 18 - 12 = 6$.
Step 2. $t_{20} = t_1 + (20 - 1) d = 12 + 19(6) = 12 + 114 = 126$.
$\boxed{\; t_{20} = 126 \;}$
Problem 2 — Given $t_3 = 10, t_5 = 18$, find $t_{40}$.
Step 1. Two unknowns ($t_1, d$). Use the gap between $t_3$ and $t_5$ — that's 2 steps:
$t_5 - t_3 = 2d \;\Rightarrow\; 18 - 10 = 2d \;\Rightarrow\; d = 4$.
Step 2. Find $t_1$: $t_3 = t_1 + 2d \Rightarrow 10 = t_1 + 8 \Rightarrow t_1 = 2$.
Step 3. $t_{40} = t_1 + 39 d = 2 + 39(4) = 2 + 156 = 158$.
$\boxed{\; t_{40} = 158 \;}$
Problem 3 — Given $t_1 = 88, t_3 = 80$, find $t_{50}$.
Step 1. Gap from $t_1$ to $t_3$ is 2 steps:
$t_3 - t_1 = 2d \;\Rightarrow\; 80 - 88 = 2d \;\Rightarrow\; d = -4$.
Step 2. $t_{50} = t_1 + 49 d = 88 + 49(-4) = 88 - 196 = -108$.
$\boxed{\; t_{50} = -108 \;}$
Note: negative $d$ means the sequence decreases — and yes, $t_{50}$ comes out negative.
Part B · Geometric Sequences
Problem 4 — Given $t_1 = 5, t_2 = 10$, find $t_{10}$.
Step 1. $r = t_2 / t_1 = 10 / 5 = 2$.
Step 2. $t_{10} = t_1 \cdot r^{10 - 1} = 5 \cdot 2^9 = 5 \cdot 512 = 2560$.
$\boxed{\; t_{10} = 2560 \;}$
Problem 5 — Given $t_2 = 27, t_4 = 3$, find $t_8$.
Step 1. Two steps apart: $t_4 / t_2 = r^2 = 3 / 27 = 1/9$.
$r = \pm \dfrac{1}{3}$ — both signs are valid; we'll see they give the same $t_8$.
Step 2. Find $t_1$ from $t_2 = t_1 \cdot r$:
Case $r = \tfrac{1}{3}$: $t_1 = 27 / (1/3) = 81$.
Case $r = -\tfrac{1}{3}$: $t_1 = 27 / (-1/3) = -81$.
Step 3. $t_8 = t_1 \cdot r^{7}$:
$r = \tfrac{1}{3}$: $t_8 = 81 \cdot (1/3)^7 = 81 / 2187 = 1/27$.
$r = -\tfrac{1}{3}$: $t_8 = -81 \cdot (-1/3)^7 = -81 \cdot (-1/2187) = 1/27$.
Both cases give the same answer (because $r^{2k} = (r^2)^k$ doesn't depend on $r$'s sign for even-index gaps, and $t_8 = t_2 \cdot r^6 = 27 \cdot (1/9)^3 = 27/729 = 1/27$).
$\boxed{\; t_8 = \dfrac{1}{27} \;}$
Problem 6 — Given $t_1 = 2, t_2 = 2^{3/2}$, find $t_{13}$.
Step 1. $r = t_2 / t_1 = 2^{3/2} / 2 = 2^{3/2 - 1} = 2^{1/2}$.
Step 2. $t_{13} = t_1 \cdot r^{12} = 2 \cdot (2^{1/2})^{12} = 2 \cdot 2^{6} = 2^{7} = 128$.
$\boxed{\; t_{13} = 128 \;}$
Why exponent rules win: $(2^{1/2})^{12} = 2^{(1/2)(12)} = 2^6$ — much faster than computing $\sqrt{2}^{12}$ as a decimal.
Part C · Series and Sums
Problem 7 — Find $S_{10}$ of the arithmetic sequence: $5, 12, 19, 26, \ldots$
Step 1. Identify $t_1 = 5$, $d = 12 - 5 = 7$.
Step 2. Find $t_{10} = t_1 + 9 d = 5 + 9(7) = 5 + 63 = 68$.
Step 3. Apply the sum formula $S_n = \dfrac{n}{2}(t_1 + t_n)$:
$S_{10} = \dfrac{10}{2}(5 + 68) = 5 \cdot 73 = 365$.
$\boxed{\; S_{10} = 365 \;}$
Problem 8 — Find $S_8$ for the geometric sequence with $t_1 = 8, r = \frac{1}{2}$.
Step 1. Apply $S_n = t_1 \cdot \dfrac{1 - r^n}{1 - r}$:
$S_8 = 8 \cdot \dfrac{1 - (1/2)^8}{1 - 1/2} = 8 \cdot \dfrac{1 - 1/256}{1/2}$
Step 2. Simplify:
$= 8 \cdot \dfrac{255/256}{1/2} = 8 \cdot \dfrac{255}{256} \cdot 2 = \dfrac{16 \cdot 255}{256} = \dfrac{255}{16}$
Step 3. Decimal form: $255/16 = 15.9375$.
$\boxed{\; S_8 = \dfrac{255}{16} = 15.9375 \;}$
Sanity check: $|r| = 1/2 < 1$, so the partial sums converge to $S_\infty = t_1/(1-r) = 8/(1/2) = 16$. After 8 terms we're at 15.9375 — within 0.0625 of the limit. ✓
Part D · Infinite Geometric Series
Problem 9 — $1 + \frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{9} + \frac{1}{27} + \cdots$
Step 1. Identify $t_1 = 1$, $r = 1/3$.
Step 2. Check $|r| = 1/3 < 1$ ✓ — series converges.
Step 3. Apply $S_\infty = \dfrac{t_1}{1 - r}$:
$S_\infty = \dfrac{1}{1 - 1/3} = \dfrac{1}{2/3} = \dfrac{3}{2}$
$\boxed{\; S_\infty = \dfrac{3}{2} \;}$
Problem 10 — Convert the repeating decimal $0.\overline{45}$ to a fraction using an infinite geometric series.
Step 1. Write the decimal as a sum:
$0.\overline{45} = 0.45 + 0.0045 + 0.000045 + \cdots$
$= \dfrac{45}{100} + \dfrac{45}{10000} + \dfrac{45}{1{,}000{,}000} + \cdots$
Step 2. Identify $t_1 = \dfrac{45}{100}$, $r = \dfrac{1}{100}$. Check $|r| < 1$ ✓.
Step 3. Apply $S_\infty = \dfrac{t_1}{1 - r}$:
$S_\infty = \dfrac{45/100}{1 - 1/100} = \dfrac{45/100}{99/100} = \dfrac{45}{99}$
Step 4. Simplify: $\dfrac{45}{99} = \dfrac{5}{11}$ (divide top & bottom by 9).
$\boxed{\; 0.\overline{45} = \dfrac{5}{11} \;}$
Verify by long division: $5 \div 11 = 0.454545\ldots = 0.\overline{45}$ ✓
